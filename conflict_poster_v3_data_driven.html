The conflict
Situation · Study group / classroom, France
Blunt feedback everyone else shrugged off
In study groups or with teachers, direct criticism that my French peers absorbed without reaction would affect me disproportionately. I'd feel targeted or diminished — while the room moved on. The data now shows why: I'm more implicit, more emotionally-wired, and more harmony-oriented than the average French person. The friction was structural, not personal.
→ Management: pause before reacting, ask "is this style or intent?", separate the information from the delivery.
Root cause · Invisible cultural gap
Neither side was wrong — the gap was real
My French peers heard "your argument is weak" as useful information. I heard it as a judgment of me. Both reactions are rational — they reflect different cultural logics about what criticism is for. My Irish-influenced values around relational care and indirect communication were colliding daily with French academic norms.
→ Resolution: understanding the gap gave me agency. I stopped asking "why are they harsh?" and started asking "why does this hit me specifically?"

Prevention strategy
Reframe before you react
When blunt feedback stings, I pause and ask: "Is this person being unkind — or are they just being French?" Separating cultural style from personal intent is the one habit that changed how I function in French academic settings. The gap is real. The conflict is structural. And knowing that is already half the solution.
